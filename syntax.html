Korean Syntax Guide

1. Basic Sentence Structure

The foundational structure of Korean sentences:

  • Subject-Object-Verb (SOV): The verb always comes at the end.
  • Example: 저는 책을 읽어요. (Jeoneun chaegeul ilg-eoyo.) - I read a book.
  • Subjects and objects are marked by particles: 이/가 (subject) and 을/를 (object).

2. Particles

Particles are essential in Korean to indicate grammatical roles:

  • Subject Particles: 이 (after consonants), 가 (after vowels)
  • Object Particles: 을 (after consonants), 를 (after vowels)
  • Location Particles: 에 (to), 에서 (at, from)
  • Topic Particle: 은/는 - Adds emphasis or contrast
  • Example: 저는 서울에 가요. (Jeoneun Seoule gayo.) - I am going to Seoul.

3. Verb Conjugation

Korean verbs are conjugated based on tense, mood, and politeness:

  • Present: -아/어/여요 (e.g., 먹다 → 먹어요)
  • Past: -았/었/였어요 (e.g., 먹다 → 먹었어요)
  • Future: -(으)ㄹ 거예요 (e.g., 먹다 → 먹을 거예요)
  • Conjugation changes based on verb stems.

4. Word Order and Modifiers

Korean word order is flexible but follows certain rules:

  • Modifiers: Adjectives and adverbs come before the noun or verb.
  • Example: 예쁜 꽃이 피었어요. (Yeppeun kkoch-i pieosseoyo.) - A pretty flower bloomed.
  • Time expressions usually come at the start of the sentence.
  • Example: 어제 제가 영화를 봤어요. (Eoje jega yeonghwareul bwasseoyo.) - Yesterday, I watched a movie.

5. Politeness Levels

Korean sentences must reflect the appropriate politeness level:

  • Casual: 반말 (e.g., 가. - Go.)
  • Polite: 존댓말 (e.g., 가요. - Go.)
  • Honorific: 높임말 (e.g., 가십니다. - Go.)

6. Sentence Types

Different sentence types in Korean:

  • Declarative: 저는 학생이에요. (Jeoneun haksaeng-ieyo.) - I am a student.
  • Interrogative: 이거 뭐예요? (Igeo mwoyeyo?) - What is this?
  • Imperative: 조용히 하세요. (Joyonghi haseyo.) - Please be quiet.
  • Propositive: 같이 갈까요? (Gachi galkkayo?) - Shall we go together?

7. Negative Sentences

How to form negative sentences:

  • 안 + Verb: 저는 공부 안 해요. (Jeoneun gongbu an haeyo.) - I don’t study.
  • Verb Stem + -지 않다: 저는 공부하지 않아요. (Jeoneun gongbuhaji anhayo.) - I don’t study.

8. Complex Sentences

Forming more complex sentences with connectors:

  • Cause: -아서/어서 (e.g., 늦어서 미안해요. - I’m sorry for being late.)
  • Contrast: -지만 (e.g., 비가 오지만 가야 해요. - It’s raining, but I have to go.)
  • Conditional: -(으)면 (e.g., 시간이 있으면 가겠습니다. - If I have time, I will go.)

9. Honorifics

Using honorifics in Korean:

  • Add -시/-으시 to verbs to show respect.
  • Example: 선생님께서 말씀하셨어요. (Seonsaengnimkkeseo malsseumhasyeosseoyo.) - The teacher spoke.

10. Practice and Common Mistakes

Practice building sentences:

  • Create sentences with different structures (e.g., negative, question, polite).
  • Avoid common mistakes such as incorrect particle usage or verb placement.
